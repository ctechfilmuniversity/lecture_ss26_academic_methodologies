 ## 2. Beyond the Tool: New Media Formats, Pedagogical Transformation, and the Politics of Digital Integration in Schools
* In this paper, I could examine how new media formats — short-form video, algorithmic feeds, participatory platforms — can be meaningfully integrated into educational systems, not as add-ons, but as tools that reshape how students think, participate, and develop critical awareness. My central argument is that simply placing TikTok videos in a classroom changes nothing fundamental; the real pedagogical shift happens when students move from passive consumers to active producers and critical readers of media. I situate this argument at the intersection of educational theory, curriculum design, and socio-political analysis, using Australia's world-first under-16 social media ban as my central case study — a political moment that crystallises the tension between protecting young people and preparing them.

* What interests me most is the gap between tool adoption and pedagogical transformation. I am not writing about whether schools should use social media — I am writing about the conditions under which engaging with new media formats actually changes how students learn. Specifically, I want to explore what it looks like when a student designs a short-form video to explain a historical event versus simply watching one, and what happens when algorithm mechanics are made visible in a classroom setting. My interest lies in that moment of conversion — from media consumption to media literacy and production — and in identifying which specific technologies and course structures make that conversion possible.
