# Task 08.01 - Introduction

## Introduction
Big tech companies are currently becoming increasingly exclusionary, technical devices are build as sealed units, so they cannot be easily opened. Tech is becoming simultaneously more corporatized*, proprietary**, and exclusive. Around the same time some kind of counter-movement is emerging: the hyperfeminine Cyberdeck Community. Women who are building cyber decks and sharing their technical knowledge, whether through documenting the process, through tutorials and workshops, or by simply explaining and showing their results in social media. The cyberdecks and the overall styles of these creators are characterized by a whimsical and cute aesthetic. They do the exact opposite of the big tech corporations: open, repairable, inviting, and made accessible through cuteness.

If tech culture isolates itself and we fail to understand counter movements that create accessibility to tech, we would miss out on recognizing the mechanisms that invite people into tech spaces. This can be relevant to design, to maker culture and regarding the question of who can contribute to tech in the future.

The whole revived retro-computing trend of „cyberdecks“, from around 40 years ago, was kicked off early this year (2026)  primarily by viral videos from Ube Boobey. She had built a small cyberdeck into a chic handbag, with physical computing, and decorated it with accessories such as gold ornaments, plastic plants, and rhinestones. In her first video about that she mentioned that she had no previous experience with tech and that it was her first try. In the following months, more and more creators appeared who built cyberdecks. And they all had something in common: their cyberdecks were kind of cute, aesthetically speaking. This is how I came to the RQ: How does cuteness operate as a mechanism of access in maker culture, and what does the hyperfeminine cyberdeck community illustrate about this? 

In this paper you will find 
- A descriptive characterization about the hyperfeminine cyberdeck community as phenomenon
- An application of cute studies to a new context: maker culture/tech
- An initial connection between two previously separate fields of research: Cute Studies (Dale, 2023) and maker culture/feminist HCI (Bardzell, 2010). Both topics already have been investigated individually but their intersection remains unexplored. The present study aims to fill this gap.

This paper combines academic literature on cute studies with the observation of publicly available social media content from the cyberdeck community, examined through a single descriptive case study and deductive content analysis.
