### Direction 3 – Style as Method
1. **Bardzell, S. (2010). Feminist HCI: Taking stock and outlining an agenda for design. ACM CHI Conference on Human Factors in Computing Systems.**

    🔗 [Link to the source](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/221513596_Feminist_HCI_Taking_stock_and_outlining_an_agenda_for_design)

    This paper argues that technology design is never neutral, it always reflects assumptions about who the user is. It is relevant because it provides the main theoretical framework for this paper: the idea that aesthetics can signal who is welcome in a space and who is not.

2. **Gatz, E., Kotturi, Y., Kwamya, A. A., & Fox, S. (2025). A Node on the Constellation: The Role of Feminist Makerspaces in Building and Sustaining Alternative Cultures of Technology Production. Proc. ACM Hum.-Comput. Interact.**

    🔗 [Link to the source](https://arxiv.org/pdf/2507.22329)

    This paper looks at feminist makerspaces, community spaces where people build and make things together outside of mainstream tech culture. It is relevant because it shows how these spaces work as alternatives to dominant tech culture, which connects directly to the hyperfeminine cyberdeck community.

3. **Capel, T. (2022). Making a Makerspace: Reimagining Makerspaces for Women's Participation. QUT Thesis.**

    🔗 [Link to the source](https://eprints.qut.edu.au/238189/1/Tara%2BCapel%2BThesis%283%29.pdf)

    This research looks at why women often feel excluded from makerspaces and how these spaces can be changed to be more welcoming. It is relevant because it directly addresses who gets included and excluded in maker culture, which is the central question of Direction 3.
