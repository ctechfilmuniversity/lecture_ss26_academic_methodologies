## Task 02.01 - Question Development

Submission: Your collection of questions, and for each their description and your motivation for them.

####  1. How do design features of interactive installations affect perceived uncertainty and resulting interaction behavior?

This question combines two aspects that are central to interactive installations: the technical/design side and the human response. The focus is not only on whether people interact, but on what role uncertainty or inhibition plays in shaping that interaction. This is interesting to me in regards of connecting behavioral outcomes (what people do) with an internal factor (how uncertain they feel). The question can be addressed through a literature-based analysis of existing studies on interactive installations, human-computer interaction, and user experience in public or immersive environments.

I would look at literature about
- existing different design features (feedback, clarity, etc)
- how incertainty or inhibition is measured in participative concepts
- how interaction behaviour is defined (participation rate, engagement time, ...)

  
**COMMENT LENA**: This is already a fairly strong and interesting question (I could use your results later ;)). However I think  "perceived uncertainty" is hardly manageable. How would you measure or analyse that? Do you want to do a user study? How about making it more specific along the lines:
- Which design features of interactive installations could influence hesitation, exploration, or withdrawal in first-time users?
- Which common design strategies do interactive installations employ to manage user orientation, and how do these shape interaction behavior? (if there are common design strategies in the related work?)
- "How do exemplary interactive media art projects manage user orientation, and which design principles do they share?" (if you, yourself, would like to analyse a couple of examples and derive design features)


#### 2. How does the way interactive installations are introduced in exhibition spaces affect visitors’ initial willingness to engage?
This question is similar to the first one, but the focus on the initial introduction to the installation is particularly intersting because first contact strongly shapes whether visitors decide to engage or not. The way an installation is introduced , either through instructions, spatial cues, or absence of guidance, is often a key but underexplored part of the overall visitor experience.
I would examine different framing structures as part of the user journey, and how they shape the transition from observation to participation. This includes looking at how narrative or instructional framing can lower hesitation and encourage more exploratory or “brave” forms of interaction in exhibition contexts.

  
**COMMENT LENA**: How to analyse / measure "initial willingness to engage"? Also, I would recommend to stay descriptive rather than explanatory. How about "How do exemplary interactive media art installations motivate audience engagement, and which design principles do they share??"



#### 3. How do design features of interactive exhibition installations influence visitors’ feelings of safety and comfort during their experience?

This direction focuses on the emotional experience at the moment visitors first encounter an installation. Whether someone decides to interact often depends less on technical understanding and more on how safe or exposed they feel in that situation. If visitors feel uncertain, watched, or afraid of making mistakes, they may avoid interaction even if the installation is accessible. The aim is to understand how specific design choices shape these feelings, elements like instructions, feedback, spatial setup, or clarity of interaction.

Directions of research:
- how are safety, anxiety, and comfort described in interactive environments?
- which design features are linked to reduced hesitation or stress?

How would you define "feelings of safety and comfort" - can you break this down (distance, enclosure, control ...)? Can you find related work for that to simply follow (alla "XY define a feeling of safety as....")(I doubt it though) Otherwise this is only possible to be answered with a user study, and with that a large project. I think as of now this topic is the least suitable one.
