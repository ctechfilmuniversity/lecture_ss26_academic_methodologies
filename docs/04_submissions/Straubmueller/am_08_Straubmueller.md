Recent advances in diffusion models expanded the opportunities and use cases for AI-generated visual content. While early diffusion systems needed seconds or even minutes to create an image, current models are close to real-time generation. This advantage is especially interesting for artists in the field of audio-visual performances, where they slowly start to integrate these systems into their workflow.

TouchDesigner is a node-based visual scripting environment that is widely used for interactive live installations and performances. It allows for the integration of other software and data from hardware components and facilitates real-time rendering and audio-reactivity. The integration of diffusion models into this workflow additionally enables artists to blend their art with AI-generated content in real-time, making visuals more versatile. This is possible through the open-source pipeline called StreamDiffusion. Instead of changing the actual AI models, StreamDiffusion changes how the data flows, using pipeline-level tactics to improve latency, fps, and visual stability.

Despite those new developments, it remains difficult to gain full control over the generated output. Existing workflows primarily rely on prompts and predefined model behavior reacting to different data inputs. While this offers some degree of customization, it stays unclear to what extent this pipeline lets artists include their individual handwriting. In comparison to other environments, Streamdiffusion doesn’t offer the integration of LoRAs by default, which further limits the visual guiding of the model.0

Fir this reason the following research question is addressed:
How effectively do the control parameters available in StreamDiffusion support artistic control over AI-generated visuals in real-time audiovisual performances?

To answer this question, a Streamdiffusion pipeline is implemented into TouchDesigner to identify and evaluate existing control methods like prompting or audio and video inputs. A further attempt is the inclusion of a custom-trained LoRA. These methods are then evaluated through qualitative observations on artistic control and also on their effects on performance, like latency and fps, through quantitative metrics.
